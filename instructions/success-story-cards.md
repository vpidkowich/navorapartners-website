# SOP: Writing & Organizing Success Story Cards for the Website

## Purpose

This document instructs an agent on how to write, structure, and sequence success story cards for a social proof section on a website. These cards sit alongside case study videos and serve as the primary scroll-stopping mechanism that motivates a visitor to click play.

The audience is direct-to-consumer ecommerce founders and operators, typically running brands between $5M–$15M in revenue, who are experiencing growth plateaus, data confusion, and profit erosion. Refer to the company ICP document for the full profile.

---

## Part 1: Writing Success Story Cards

### Card Anatomy

Each card consists of exactly 5 elements:

1. **Headline** — the scroll-stopper
2. **Bullet 1** — industry + locality
3. **Bullet 2** — unique credibility signal
4. **Bullet 3** — the challenge (in ICP language)
5. **Bullet 4** — secondary proof point or speed indicator

### Headline Rules

The headline is the single most important element. It must contain:

- **A concrete number.** Dollar figures beat percentages. If dollar figures are available, use them in the headline and move the percentage to a bullet point. If only a percentage or ratio is available, lead with that.
- **A clear company type descriptor.** This is what differentiates one card from another at a glance. The reader should be able to scan 8 headlines and instantly know which story is relevant to them without reading any bullets.

**Headline formula:** `[Most Significant Result] + [Company Type Differentiator]`

**Examples:**
- "Auto Parts Revenue from $280K to $1.5M/Month" — dollar figures + company type
- "3x Revenue for a Handmade Jewelry Brand" — multiplier + company type
- "$98 nCAC Drop for an Australian Air Purifier Brand" — dollar figure + locality + company type
- "30% ROI Boost for a Neurofeedback Device Brand" — percentage + specific product category

**Headline principles:**
- When dollar figures exist, they go in the headline. Percentages move to the final bullet.
- When only percentages or ratios exist, lead with those.
- Use "3x" not "tripling." Use "$98 drop" not "significant reduction." Specificity always wins.
- The company descriptor should be specific enough to differentiate but generic enough to not reveal the brand name. "Auto parts retailer" works. "Suspension and shocks retailer" is too narrow and risks identification.
- No two headlines across the full set should sound alike. If multiple cards feature revenue growth, vary the framing: one uses dollar-to-dollar, another uses a multiplier, another leads with the efficiency metric instead.
- Use the language of the ICP. Terms like "nCAC," "MER," "ROI" signal fluency and filter for sophisticated buyers. Do not dumb down the metrics.
- Be specific about the product/device category. If the product is a neurofeedback device, call it a "neurofeedback device" — not a "smart meditation wearable." Specificity lends credibility to the audience that matters. Genericizing makes it sound like consumer lifestyle fluff.

### Bullet Point Rules

Each bullet is 5–8 words. No sentences. No punctuation-heavy constructions. These are scan-targets, not paragraphs.

**Bullet 1 — Industry + Locality:**
Purpose: Instant self-selection. The reader sees "DTC organic skincare brand, Alabama, USA" and either thinks "that's my world" or moves to the next card.
- Always start with "DTC" to signal direct-to-consumer.
- Include city/state/country. People relate to locality — it builds trust and signals "this works in my market."
- Format: `DTC [product category], [Location]`

**Bullet 2 — Unique Credibility Signal:**
Purpose: Builds authority and makes this case study memorable. This is the "wow, they're legit" moment.
- What makes this company distinctive? Massive SKU count, celebrity endorsements, category pioneer status, sold in major retail chains, used by hospitals/government, etc.
- This bullet should make the reader think "if they can handle THAT complexity, they can handle mine."
- Format: Short, declarative fact. No fluff.

**Bullet 3 — The Challenge:**
Purpose: Mirror the reader's pain. This bullet should make them feel seen.
- Name the problem using ICP language: "entire inventory trapped in one campaign," "PMax cannibalizing branded search," "broken tracking confused the algorithm," "inflated conversion data masked real performance."
- Avoid generic language like "struggling to scale" or "needed help with ads." Be operationally specific.
- The reader should be able to point at this bullet and say "that's exactly what's happening to us."

**Bullet 4 — Secondary Proof Point / Speed Indicator:**
Purpose: Reinforces the headline with a complementary metric and/or shows how fast results arrived.
- If the headline uses dollar figures, this bullet uses a percentage or efficiency metric.
- If the headline uses a percentage, this bullet uses dollar figures or a timeframe.
- Including a time indicator ("in 90 days," "across 24 months," "in peak season") adds urgency and credibility.
- This is where percentages that got bumped from the headline land.

### What NOT to Do

- Do not reveal company names anywhere. Privacy is non-negotiable.
- Do not use more than 4 bullets. Each additional bullet dilutes attention.
- Do not write bullets longer than 8 words unless absolutely necessary.
- Do not use generic marketing language ("amazing results," "huge improvement," "game-changing"). Use numbers.
- Do not repeat the same metric format across multiple cards. Variety in how results are expressed keeps the section scannable.
- Do not bash competitors, agencies, or platforms in the bullets. State the problem factually. "PMax cannibalizing branded search" is factual. "Previous agency was incompetent" is not.

## Part 2: Organizing Cards on the Page

### Layout Context

Cards are displayed in a 2-column grid, 4 rows, 8 cards total. Each card sits beside a video. The reader scans left-to-right, then moves down a row.

### Sequencing Principles

**Row-level rule: Pair contrasting stories on every row.**
The left card and right card on any row should feel like different stories. If the left card is a big revenue scaling story, the right card should be an efficiency or data-fix story. This prevents the section from feeling repetitive no matter where the reader's eye lands.

**Contrast categories:**
- Revenue growth / scaling stories (big dollar figures, percentage growth)
- Efficiency / cost reduction stories (nCAC drops, ROI improvements)
- Data/tracking fix stories (fake data exposed, broken tracking repaired)
- Structural simplification stories (account cleanup, consolidation)

Never place two cards from the same category side by side.

**Vertical sequencing (top to bottom):**

- **Row 1 — The Opening Punch:** Lead with the two most impressive transformations. These must stop the scroll. Dollar figures and large multipliers belong here. This is the "holy shit" row.
- **Row 2 — Scale Meets Precision:** Pair a scaling story with a surgical-fix story. This row proves you can do both — grow big AND fix what's broken.
- **Row 3 — Competitive Wins:** Feature stories from competitive niches, international markets, or prestigious client types. This row builds breadth of credibility.
- **Row 4 — Smart Fixes, Big Gains:** Close with "less is more" stories that reinforce strategic thinking over brute-force spending. These resonate deeply with the ICP's desire for systems over tactics.

**Priority scoring for card placement:**
When deciding which cards go in Row 1 vs Row 4, score each card on:

1. **Dollar magnitude** — Larger absolute numbers rank higher.
2. **Percentage magnitude** — Bigger percentage swings rank higher.
3. **ICP pain alignment** — Stories that mirror the most common ICP pain (data confusion, scaling plateau, profit erosion) rank higher.
4. **Company type relatability** — DTC ecom brands rank higher than adjacent industries.
5. **Speed of result** — Faster time-to-result ranks higher.

Cards scoring highest across these dimensions go in Row 1. Cards scoring lowest (but still strong) go in Row 4.

### Geographic & Industry Diversity

Distribute locations across the grid. Avoid clustering all US-based stories in one row. If you have an international case study, pair it with a domestic one. This signals global capability without making international markets feel like an afterthought.

Similarly, spread product categories. Don't place two skincare brands or two high-ticket items on the same row.

## Part 3: Quality Checklist

Before finalizing the card set, verify:

- [ ] Every headline contains a concrete number
- [ ] Every headline contains a company type descriptor
- [ ] No two headlines sound alike in structure or framing
- [ ] Every Bullet 1 includes "DTC" + product category + location
- [ ] Every Bullet 2 states something unique about the company
- [ ] Every Bullet 3 names a specific operational challenge
- [ ] Every Bullet 4 provides a complementary metric or time indicator
- [ ] No bullets exceed ~8 words
- [ ] No company names appear anywhere
- [ ] Each row pairs contrasting story types (left vs right)
- [ ] Row 1 contains the two most impressive transformations
- [ ] Geographic and industry variety is distributed across all 4 rows
- [ ] Language matches ICP terminology (nCAC, MER, P&L, PMax, etc.)
- [ ] No agency-bashing or competitor-bashing language appears
- [ ] Product/device categories are specific, not genericized
